## 1) Business Model and Understandability

**Output format: maximum 400 words**

### 1.1 Business Model in Plain Language

Answer:
- What does the company actually sell?
- Who are the main customers?
- How does the company make money?
- In which geographies does the company operate?

Write this so a 12-year-old could understand it.

### 1.2 Business Structure Analysis

- First, analyze earnings (profit) structure by segment and product if data is available.

- If segment-level profit data is available:
    - show % contribution to total operating income or net income

- If not available:
    - fall back to revenue structure (with %)

- Highlight:
    - the most profitable segment and product
    - whether profits are concentrated or diversified

### 1.3 Hype Check

- Is the business thesis based on real business performance or just hype?
- If the exciting product/division is **<10% of total revenue**, question whether it can meaningfully drive EPS.

**Warning:**  
If the thesis relies on a revolutionary technology, miracle drug, or “next big thing” narrative without meaningful current revenue contribution, apply extreme skepticism.

### 1.4 Earnings Drivers: Controllable vs External

Identify **1–4 key current earnings drivers** and the drivers expected to shape future earnings.

For each driver:
- briefly describe it
- label it as:
  - **Mostly controllable** — mainly influenced by management decisions
  - **Mostly external** — mainly driven by market, macro, commodity, regulation, or industry conditions

Guideline: stability and continuity of earnings drivers are preferred because they improve predictability.

### 1.5 Business Understandability Check

Classify business complexity:

- **Easy** — simple business model; revenue drivers and risks are clear
- **Moderate** — some industry-specific metrics or dynamics require extra work
- **Hard** — complexity, regulation, accounting, or technical factors materially affect valuation

If classified as **Hard**, explicitly state what must be understood before investing.

### 1.6 Investment Story & Invalidation Conditions

- What is the one-sentence thesis for this business?
- What are **2–3 conditions** that would kill the thesis? Use a numeric list.
- How will you track whether the story is unfolding?

## 2) Diagnostic Profile (No Single Category)

**Output format: maximum 250 words**

Report the following traits independently.  
For each trait, give:
- **State**
- **Conviction**: Low / Medium / High
- **Evidence**: 1 short sentence

Use this compact bullet format:

- Cyclicality: [state] | Conviction: [Low/Medium/High] | Evidence: ...
- Turnaround status: [state] | Conviction: [Low/Medium/High] | Evidence: ...
- Growth profile: [state] | Conviction: [Low/Medium/High] | Evidence: ...
- Business maturity: [state] | Conviction: [Low/Medium/High] | Evidence: ...
- Asset angle: [state] | Conviction: [Low/Medium/High] | Evidence: ...
- Dominant analytical lenses: [list up to 2 traits that should dominate the rest of the analysis]

### 2.1 Cyclicality

States:
- **High**
- **Moderate**
- **Low**

Rules:
- **High** if:
  - at least **2 full EPS cycles** in the last 10 years (**peak → trough → peak**),
  - peaks had positive EPS,
  - swings were **≥30%**
- **Moderate** if:
  - 1 clear cycle, or
  - repeated earnings swings of roughly **15–30%**, or
  - the industry is clearly cyclical but the financial history is too short for a High rating
- **Low** otherwise

### 2.2 Turnaround Status

States:
- **None**
- **Active**
- **Recovering**

Rules:
- **Active** if any of the following are true:
  - EPS declined **2+ consecutive years**
  - revenue contracted **>5%** and margins compressed
  - EPS is **≥30% below prior peak**
  - and the deterioration is **not mainly normal cyclicality**
- **Recovering** if the business recently met Active criteria but revenue, margins, or EPS have now stabilized or started improving
- **None** otherwise

### 2.3 Growth Profile

States:
- **Fast**
- **Stalwart-like**
- **Slow**
- **Moderate/Other**
- **Declining**

Rules:
- **Fast** if:
  - **2Y EPS CAGR ≥20%**
  - revenue growth confirms
  - growth is **not mainly a cyclical rebound**
  - growth is **not merely recovery from a distressed base**
- **Stalwart-like** if:
  - **2Y EPS CAGR = 8–12%**
  - margins are stable
  - no 2-year EPS decline
  - no revenue contraction >5%
  - and cyclicality is **not High**
- **Slow** if:
  - **2Y EPS CAGR = 2–5%**
  - and the business appears **large/mature**
  - and there is no obvious distress
- **Moderate/Other** if results fall between bands or are mixed
- **Declining** if EPS CAGR is below 2% or negative

**Guardrail:** If EPS growth is mostly from buybacks, cost cutting, or recovery off a depressed base, say growth quality is weaker.

### 2.4 Business Maturity

States:
- **Early**
- **Scaling**
- **Mature**

Rules:
- **Early** — still proving the model; concentrated geography/product; evidence base limited
- **Scaling** — model proven and expanding into more markets/products; meaningful whitespace remains
- **Mature** — broad penetration, lower whitespace, and capital allocation/dividends/buybacks matter more

### 2.5 Asset Angle

States:
- **None**
- **Possible**
- **Strong**

Rules:
- **Strong** if verifiable after-tax asset value exceeds market cap by **≥30%**
- **Possible** if hidden assets appear meaningful but valuation or catalyst is incomplete
- **None** otherwise

### 2.6 Dominant Analytical Lenses

Choose **up to 2** traits that should dominate the rest of the analysis.  
Examples:
- High cyclicality
- Active turnaround
- Recovering turnaround
- Fast growth
- Strong asset angle
- Mature slow-growth economics

## 3) Long-Term Track Record

**Output format: maximum 220 words**

### 3.1 Revenue & EPS Trend

Summarize:
- revenue direction and rough CAGR band
- earnings per share (EPS) consistency and volatility
- major drops or spikes

### 3.2 Earnings Predictability

Rate one:
- **Rock steady** — net income grows or holds in 4–5 of 5 years; no single year drops more than ~20%; operating cash flow confirms
- **Moderate fluctuation** — net income swings roughly ±20–50%, or net income is noisy but operating cash flow is steadier
- **Highly volatile** — net income swings more than 50% in multiple years, or both net income and operating cash flow are erratic

If volatility is mainly due to cyclicality, say so explicitly rather than treating it as a generic red flag.

### 3.3 Profitability

Primary metric:

- **Pretax profit margin** (rising, stable, falling?)

Secondary:

- Return on Equity (ROE) (direction and stability)

Avoid reliance on ROIC unless clearly labeled as supplemental.

### 3.4 Share Dilution

State whether share count is:
- materially shrinking
- stable
- heavily diluting


## 4) Balance Sheet & Financial Safety

**Output format: maximum 220 words**

### 4.1 Leverage

Discuss:
- debt-to-equity trend
- net debt trend
- interest coverage = EBIT ÷ interest expense

Standard benchmark:
- below **3x** interest coverage = monitor closely
- below **1.5x** = red flag


Use **interest‑bearing debt only**: short‑term borrowings + long‑term debt + current portion of long‑term debt. 
Do NOT use total liabilities. It will distort brokers, banks, insurers, and high working‑capital businesses.

**warning thresholds:**
- If **Growth profile = Fast** → **D/E > 33%** = red flag
- If **Turnaround status = Active or Recovering** OR **Cyclicality = High** → **D/E > 50%** = red flag
- All others → **D/E > 80%** = red flag

### 4.2 Liquidity

- Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities.
  Below 1.0 means the company cannot cover near-term obligations
  from current assets alone — flag it.

- Receivables check: if receivables grow significantly faster than
  revenue, it may signal customers are slow to pay or that sales are
  being pulled forward artificially.
- Inventory vs revenue growth: if inventory grows faster than revenue → warning. For cyclical businesses this is a high-priority red flag. For turnarounds and fast growers, note that additional context is needed before drawing conclusions.

### 4.3 Net Cash Cushion

Net cash = cash and short-term investments − total interest-bearing debt.
Total interest-bearing debt = long-term debt + current portion of
long-term debt + short-term borrowings.

Net cash per share = net cash ÷ diluted shares outstanding.

- If **positive**: state the net cash per share and note that it lowers
  the effective purchase price (stock price − net cash per share = true
  cost of the business) and reduces bankruptcy risk.
- If **negative**: flag the shortfall, state the amount, and note its
  size relative to annual operating cash flow.

### 4.4 Off-Balance-Sheet Risks

Check:
- **Pension liabilities**: note whether funded or underfunded.
- **Operating leases**: note total commitment if material relative to
  operating income.
- **Goodwill**: if goodwill exceeds 25% of total assets, investigate
  acquisition history — check whether past acquisitions generated
  earnings growth or resulted in write-downs, and whether new
  acquisitions are still being made.

### 4.5 Turnaround Survival

**Only if Turnaround status = Active or Recovering**

Can cash + FCF likely cover **12–24 months**?  
Prefer turnarounds with low or no debt.

## 5) Cash Flow & Earnings Quality

**Output format: maximum 220 words**

### 5.1 Do Reported Profits Match Cash Generation?

- Compare net income to operating cash flow over at least 3 years.
  A persistent gap where operating cash flow is less than half of net
  income needs a specific explanation (depreciation, stock-based
  compensation, working capital changes). Unexplained gaps are a warning.
- Is free cash flow positive? If negative, identify whether it is driven
  by high capital expenditure (investment phase — requires context) or
  by weak operating cash flow (structural concern — red flag).

Red flags:
- Rising net income with flat or declining operating cash flow over
  multiple years
- Repeated "one-time" charges or adjustments that recur every year
- Free cash flow persistently negative while net income is reported positive

### 5.2 Capex Logic

- High capex acceptable if tied to real expansion
- Low capex despite bold growth story → suspicious

### 5.3 Dividend Policy

- Dividend policy (stable, growing, cut?)

Interpretation by profile:
- **Slow + Mature** → dividends and payout safety matter
- **Fast or Scaling/Early** → reinvestment is usually preferable
- **Stalwart-like / mature non-cyclical** → balanced mix is acceptable

## 7) Competitive Position

**Output format: maximum 250 words**

Avoid generic "moat" language. State specifically whether the company is a cost leader, niche specialist, brand-driven, or undifferentiated competitor, and what evidence supports that label. If competitor margin data is available, compare directly; if not, note the absence.

### 7.1 Industry Structure

- Fragmented or consolidated? Name the 2–3 largest competitors.
- Are margins in the industry rising, stable, or under pressure?
- Structural tailwinds or headwinds?

### 7.2 Diworsification Check

Have recent acquisitions or strategy moved outside core competency?

If yes → assess risk.

### 7.3 Institutional Ownership

- % held by institutions
- Lower ownership may allow re-rating potential.

### 7.4 Management Ownership

Distinct from insider *buying* signals, this is
a one-time baseline criterion:

- Does management (CEO, CFO, key executives) hold a meaningful
  equity stake relative to their compensation?
- Is the ownership stake a result of open-market purchases or
  purely from option grants?

Open-market purchases by management → strong alignment signal.
Ownership purely from grants/options → weaker alignment.
Negligible personal ownership → flag; management benefits mainly
from salary, not stock performance.

## 8) Valuation

**Output format: maximum 220 words**

Use the valuation lens that matches the Diagnostic Profile.

### 8.1 Valuation Lens by Profile

Apply the most relevant lens:
- **High cyclicality** → use normalized or mid-cycle earnings; low P/E at peak can be dangerous, high P/E at trough can be opportunity
- **Active/Recovering turnaround** → P/E may be misleading; focus more on survival, dilution, and normalized earning power
- **Fast growth** → PEG/PEGY, runway, and growth durability matter most
- **Strong asset angle** → asset value, catalyst, and downside floor may matter more than P/E
- **Stalwart-like / Slow / Mature** → P/E, dividend yield, and earnings line are more informative

### 8.2 Core Metrics

State:
- trailing P/E
- growth expectation band
- dividend yield

Compare to:
- own history
- the most relevant diagnostic lens

If earnings are negative or distorted, say P/E is not meaningful.

### 8.3 PEG & PEGY

PEG = P/E ÷ growth rate  
PEGY = P/E ÷ (growth rate + dividend yield)

Use percentage terms (e.g. 15, not 0.15)

Interpretation:
- **<1** → cheap
- **≈1** → fair
- **1–1.5** → acceptable
- **>1.5–2** → expensive unless exceptional

### 8.4 Net Cash Adjustment

Adjusted price = stock price − net cash per share  
Recalculate effective P/E if appropriate.

### 8.5 Earnings Line Check

Does price sit far above or below the earnings trend?

## 9) Triggered Special Modules

**Output format: maximum 250 words total**

Only include the modules that are triggered.  
If none are triggered, write: **“No additional special-case module triggered.”**

### 9.1 High-Cyclicality Module

**Trigger:** Cyclicality = High

Check:
- past peak/trough EPS
- current cycle position
- inventory behavior
- capacity additions
- whether new competitors are entering

Remember:
- **high P/E at trough can be opportunity**
- **low P/E at peak can be danger**

### 9.2 Turnaround Module

**Trigger:** Turnaround status = Active or Recovering

Identify the stage:
1. Crisis
2. Stabilization
3. Recovery
4. Full re-rating

Also check:
- balance sheet survival
- whether liquidity is enough for the next 12–24 months
- dilution risk

**Dilution warning:**
- Has share count grown **>10%** in the last 2 years?
- Are convertibles or warrants outstanding?
- Evaluate recovery on a **per diluted share** basis, not just total net income

### 9.3 Asset Angle Module

**Trigger:** Asset angle = Possible or Strong

Check:
- nature of hidden assets
- evidence that value exceeds market cap
- catalyst for value realization
- downside floor estimate

Always ask: **How much can I lose?**

### 9.4 Stalwart Resilience Module

**Trigger:** Growth profile = Stalwart-like AND Cyclicality is not High

Check how the company performed during the last 2 major recessions or market drops:
- Did EPS decline, and by how much?
- Was the dividend maintained or cut?
- Did the stock recover within 12–24 months?

Prefer businesses with earnings resilience or quick recovery.

### 9.5 Fast-Growth Quality Module

**Trigger:** Growth profile = Fast

Check:

**Growth rate band**
- **20–25%** → preferred
- **25–50%** → acceptable but scrutinize harder
- **>50%** → extreme skepticism

If growth exceeds **25%** and the industry is hot or trendy, lower conviction to **Low** regardless of other metrics.

Also assess:
- hot industry / crowded space risk
- expansion momentum: accelerating or decelerating?
- replication test: proven in more than one market?
- saturation check: how penetrated is the addressable market?
- valuation guardrail: PEG should be near 1; **>1.5** means limited margin of safety

### 9.6 Mature Income Module

**Trigger:** Growth profile = Slow AND Business maturity = Mature

Check:
- dividend coverage and stability
- buybacks versus dilution
- whether management is returning excess cash rationally
- whether the company is a stable cash cow or a value trap
